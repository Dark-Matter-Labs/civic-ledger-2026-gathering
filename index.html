01 · The underlying claim

What this work is actually about

Cities make decisions every day about what is allowed and what is not. A market in a car park. A shared meal in an empty shop. A night of music in a courtyard. Each one needs permission. Most of the time the answer is no, not because the thing is harmful, but because it does not fit the box.

Permission is not a small bureaucratic step. It is the operating system of the city. It decides what is possible before anything else can begin. Every policy, every service, every attempt to make a city more alive runs into the question of whether the permission structure will allow it. Change that structure, and you change what a city can do.

Power is the ability to shape a shared future together. Negotiation is how we get there. And what makes a commitment real is that something was given up to make it.

A booking is not a commitment. A conditional agreement reached through negotiation, trade-offs, and the backing of neighbours is. That difference is the whole point. Civic Ledger is not a booking platform. It is a new way for a city to run, one where people negotiate, commit, and shape the places they share.

02 · Core arguments

Eleven things we hold to be true

Some of these are well evidenced. Some are tested but not yet written up. Some are still assumptions we need the living lab to confirm. The tags say which is which, honestly.

1

Permission is the operating system of the city Argued

Not an add-on. The layer that decides what is possible. Every policy or tool introduced into a city is held back by what the permission structure allows.

2

Today's systems record the answer, not the reasoning Evidenced

A yes or a no is logged. The conditions, the worries, the context all vanish. The next similar request starts from nothing. The city cannot learn from itself.

3

Discretion without clear rules breeds inconsistency Evidenced

When the rules do not cover a case, the officer picks the safe answer. The same proposal gets a different result depending on who handles it and when.

4

New uses are stopped before anyone tests if they work Evidenced

Activities that do not fit a category are treated as exceptions. Community-led, temporary, and mixed uses are halted, not because they are risky, but because they are unclassified.

5

Technology laid over a broken structure does not fix it Argued

Cities have added digital tools for decades. The way decisions get made underneath barely changed. The problem was never a shortage of technology.

6

The shift is from "is this allowed?" to "what would make this possible?" Partly tested

Conditional agreements instead of a flat yes or no. Conditions that are specific, open to change, and written down. The question moves from gatekeeping to designing access together.

7

A commitment is real because something was given up to make it Core claim

A confirmation is not a commitment. An agreement reached through negotiation, trade-offs, and a neighbour's backing is. This is what sets Civic Ledger apart from any tool that simply hands out approvals.

8

The system flow is a cycle, not a procedure Partly tested

Six steps, from intent to learning, that are not a fixed line. Each case feeds back into the next. The system learns from every decision it helps make.

9

Trust and learning build up over time Assumption

Reputation, a record of care, the backing of peers: these add up and lower the friction next time. Rules change through use, not through a rewrite by administrators. The longer it runs, the more useful it gets.

10

Risk can be shared and lowered, not just judged Partly tested

A proposer can reduce risk by adjusting their own plan or by drawing on community support. What risk is left becomes a small surcharge that funds local resilience. Risk is a material to work with, not only a filter.

11

This is built to be adopted, not just used Future claim

Cities can adapt, fork, and extend it. The aim is shared infrastructure for civic decisions that any town can build on, not a single product owned by one company.

03 · The investor lens

What civic tech funders are looking for

Funders backing this kind of infrastructure are not asking whether the idea is good. They have seen good ideas. They are asking whether it works with real people in real places, whether the team knows what broke and why, and whether the system is learning fast enough to scale.

From the conversation with Caine on 28 May 2026: roughly a fifth of what an investor needs is the problem and the solution. The other three fifths is evidence that the platform works and will grow. That evidence is not in the blogs yet. This is where we start building it.

Question one highest weight

Does real demand exist?

Not "cities need this." Instead: of the spaces registered in Incheon, how many got a proposal in the first four weeks? Did anyone come back to propose a second time?

Question two

Does it work as designed?

Can each role, the space owner, the organiser, the peer group, the participant, finish their task without help? Where do people stop? Where do they ask what to do next?

Question three hardest, most important

What did you learn, and what did you change?

Not what the platform does. What the team found out by building and testing it, and what they rebuilt because of what they found.

Question four

Does it hold across different places?

The living lab runs across three different districts in Incheon. Where does behaviour line up? Where does it differ? What lines up is the transferable core.

Question five

Is there a human story?

One organiser. One space owner. One person from the city. What did this make possible that would not have happened otherwise?

04 · Assumptions and evidence

What we assumed, what broke, what changed

The team expected to iterate. Here is what actually changed. Commitments are living decision premises: we made them, we tested them, and we renegotiated them as we learned.

In hand: documented in sprint records and meeting notes Incoming: from the living lab, June to August 2026
Assumption 01
Four fixed roles map cleanly onto how people use the system.
How we tested

Sprint 1 space registration, Sprint 2 deliberation flow, and the usability test with nine people in May 2026.

What we found

Roles are not fixed. People move between them across a project. The peer group was doing coordination work we had not designed for. The system is really about many roles working together, not single actions.

What changed

Role design is being reworked. The English version needs dynamic role swapping, not static assignment. This is feature one on the redesign list.

Assumption 02
The AI can offer genuinely creative compromise options when a proposal needs negotiation.
How we tested

Sprint 2 deliberation flow, built with an AI company partner over several weeks.

What we found

The AI kept suggesting answers that fit the existing rules. It nudged people back toward what was already allowed, rather than helping them find new ground. We had no reference data for what an imaginative compromise looks like.

What changed

This is a known open problem. The compromise engine needs a different training approach, possibly the knowledge-graph wiki Gerdin is building. Flagged for the English version.

Assumption 03
A free-flowing conversational interface is the right way to handle proposals.
How we tested

Contracted an AI company to build it. January 2026.

What we found

The company could not deliver. Free-flow conversation needed training data that did not exist. We moved to a form: people fill in their excitement, concern, and suggestion, and the AI sums it up.

What changed

The interface became form-based. The decision logic underneath, risk scoring and conditional agreement, stayed the same. We treat the form as an interim step, not the final design.

Assumption 04
Bigger discounts for higher-reputation users is the right payment model.
How we tested

Sprint 2 payment model design, late 2025.

What we found

Large reputation discounts meant almost no money came into the platform. It was not sustainable. It also risked turning the system into a game where people chase badges instead of caring for places.

What changed

Two parts now: a small reputation discount of 1 to 3 percent, and a risk surcharge of 0 to 5 percent. Leftover risk funds a community pool and a risk pool. More use means more capacity to manage risk, not just a lower price.

Assumption 05
People will feel deliberation as meaningful, not as a chore.
How we tested

Sprint 2 build, internal assessment, and reflection in December 2025.

What we found

Strong on the page, heavy in the hand. People are used to Airbnb and Booking.com. Next to those, the deliberation step felt like a burden. The May usability test showed people warmed to it once inside, but getting them in was the friction.

What changed

This is the core design challenge for 2026. The proposed answer is a different entry point: a physical object or spatial prompt that starts from imagination, not a booking calendar. See scenario three.

Assumption 06
Building the risk scoring engine would be straightforward.
How we tested

Sprint 2, several weeks of design with an AI company partner.

What we found

Reading risk from the language of a proposal, a yoga class for five against a night market for two hundred, needed far more detailed parameter work than we expected. Five weeks just to design the baseline calculation.

What changed

We have a working engine, and a clear lesson: the technical depth of civic AI is higher than it looks. This shaped the choice to hire a product architect with an AI background.

Incoming · living lab
What the living lab will tell us by end of July.
Being collected

Activation rate (registered spaces that get a proposal), completion rate, return rate, and at least one full case from proposal to conditional agreement.

Who holds it

Professor Kim's team runs the lab across three Incheon districts. A brief has been sent setting out exactly what to record as the lab runs, not after it ends.

Why it matters for Berlin

This is the demand and usability evidence funders weight most. Without it designed in from the start, neither the blog nor the gathering can do its job.

05 · The system flow

Six steps that learn

This is not a protocol. A protocol is a fixed procedure you follow. This is a flow that learns. Each step feeds the next, and the last step feeds back to the first.

Six steps
Step 01

Intent

A citizen, steward or organisation signals what they want to do, and why.

Today's systems skip this. Intent never reaches the record.

Step 02

Proposal

The request, written in everyday words, becomes a structured and reviewable case.

Not asking for a yes, but opening a conversation.

Step 03

Conditions

Rules, location, timing and local context are used to weigh risk and route the case.

Risk is read from context, not looked up from a category.

Step 04

Agreement

Conditions for use are shaped through structured negotiation where needed.

The commitment is earned through give and take, not simply granted.

Step 05

Proof

The agreement is recorded as a verifiable and auditable commitment.

The record exists. The reasoning is kept. The city can learn from it.

Step 06

Learning

Outcomes from real use inform future rules and workflows, feeding the next request.

The next request starts from a better place than this one did.

07 · Three futures

What this could become

Three short scenarios. They are not predictions. They show where the work points, and they double as design briefs for the English version. Each one is someone giving something up to make a commitment real.

Scenario one · more-than-human permissioning

The tree sends you an invitation

When the city operates through invitation, not paperwork.
01

In the park near your home, the oldest trees carry small sensors. They read light, water, warmth, and the movement of birds.

02

One afternoon the data lines up. Sun after rain. A rainbow forming at the right angle. Birdsong at its peak.

03

The tree sends you an invitation. Not an alert. An invitation, in the tree's own voice, asking you to come and sit and watch.

04

You say yes. The permission is already handled. The tree invited you into its space. You only had to come.

05

This is licensing turned around. Not "are you allowed to be here?" but "will you come and be here with me?"

Scenario two · arriving without a history

Amara's first gathering

A fair way in for someone the system has never met.
01

Amara arrived in the city in October. She wants to cook a meal for twenty people in the community garden, with a little music.

02

In the old system she would have to prove she belongs. Documents. References. A track record she does not have yet.

03

Here, the platform shows her the conditions for a gathering her size. She can see what other first-time hosts did, and what worked.

04

The risk is medium: evening, outdoors, music. The system asks one thing. Can a neighbour or a local group co-sign the safety conditions with her?

05

A resident who has hosted ten events in that garden adds their backing. The gathering is approved, with conditions. Amara cooks.

06

Afterwards she uploads the photos of a clean, tidy garden. Her first record of care is logged. Next time, the conditions are lighter.

Scenario three · imagination before booking

The object changes the question

Start from what you could imagine, not what is free at 3pm.
01

On a corner in Melbourne, someone places an odd object. A low wooden platform. A net between two poles. A ring of small stools.

02

It has no set purpose. It has a shape, and a question. What could happen here?

03

You scan it with your phone. The platform does not show you a calendar. It shows you what you could make of this spot.

04

You choose a use. The system checks the conditions, weighs the risk, and routes it. Six minutes later you have a two-hour permission for a table tennis game.

05

Three weeks on, the platform sees a pattern. Fourteen proposals on this one corner in a month. The city takes note. The object stays.

Why Berlin, why now

Berlin is not the obvious choice. It is the right one. The German funder landscape — public technology funds, civic infrastructure investors, municipal innovation programmes — is where the next tranche of Civic Ledger support is most likely to come from. Indy named Berlin explicitly on the 21 May Mission Pathways call as the place to be for this conversation.

The timing matters too. By 21 July the living lab will have run for six weeks across three Incheon sites. The team will have demand signals, at least one end-to-end case, and early usability data. That is the evidence base the gathering is designed to present. Arriving in September would mean the grant has expired and the evidence window has closed.

Overall goals
Goal 01

Design the business model

Move from a working hypothesis to a model the team can test and pitch. Who pays, how much, under what conditions, and what does the city get in return.

Goal 02

Build funder pathways

Leave Berlin with 5–10 warm contacts who can be approached with a direct ask. The Brussels public technology fund is one specific lead to identify before invitations go out.

Goal 03

Test the platform live

Use the ECS framework as a live internal tool during the gathering itself. The LLM synthesises positions in the room. The participants experience the platform from the inside.
